# History of the UK Home Office

The Home Office is one of the oldest and most significant departments of the British Government, responsible for immigration, security, law and order.

---

## Origins

The Home Office was established in 1782 when the responsibilities of the Southern Department were reorganised. Thomas Townshend, 1st Viscount Sydney, became the first Home Secretary. The department took on responsibility for domestic affairs in England and Wales, while foreign affairs passed to the Foreign Office.

Before 1782, the Secretaries of State divided their duties geographically (Northern and Southern Departments) rather than by function. The restructuring created a clearer separation between domestic and foreign policy.

---

## The role of Home Secretary

The Home Secretary (formally the Secretary of State for the Home Department) is one of the Great Offices of State. The role carries responsibility for:

- Immigration and passports
- Counter-terrorism and national security
- Policing and fire services
- Drug policy
- Licensing and regulation

The Home Secretary sits in Cabinet and is typically one of the most senior ministers in government.

---

## Key milestones

### 18th century

- **1782** — Home Office created from the Southern Department. Thomas Townshend becomes first Home Secretary.
- **1789** — French Revolution increases focus on domestic security and public order.
- **1792** — Middlesex Justices Act begins professionalisation of policing in London.

### 19th century

- **1829** — Metropolitan Police established by Home Secretary Robert Peel. The force becomes the model for modern policing.
- **1842** — Detective branch formed within the Metropolitan Police.
- **1870** — Naturalisation Act brings citizenship and nationality under Home Office control.
- **1871** — Responsibility for prisons transferred to the Home Office from local authorities.
- **1878** — Criminal Investigation Department (CID) created.

### Early 20th century

- **1905** — Aliens Act introduces immigration controls for the first time, giving the Home Office a role in managing who enters the country.
- **1916** — MI5 placed under Home Office oversight for domestic security matters.
- **1919** — Sex Disqualification (Removal) Act opens civil service (including Home Office roles) to women.
- **1920** — Official Secrets Act strengthened, expanding Home Office powers during wartime.

### Mid 20th century

- **1948** — British Nationality Act creates "Citizen of the United Kingdom and Colonies" status. HMT Empire Windrush arrives.
- **1953** — Royal prerogative of mercy (pardons) formally delegated to the Home Secretary.
- **1962** — Commonwealth Immigrants Act introduces controls on Commonwealth migration.
- **1967** — Sexual Offences Act partially decriminalises homosexuality in England and Wales (Home Office policy area).
- **1968** — Race Relations Act gives the Home Office responsibility for community relations and anti-discrimination.

### Late 20th century

- **1971** — Immigration Act creates the legal framework still largely in use today. Introduces "right of abode."
- **1985** — Handsworth and Broadwater Farm riots prompt reviews of policing and community relations.
- **1986** — Public Order Act modernises law on protests, marches, and assemblies.
- **1989** — Security Service Act places MI5 on a statutory footing under Home Office authority.
- **1997** — Home Office takes on responsibility for the newly created Crime and Disorder partnerships.
- **1998** — Human Rights Act (Home Office-sponsored legislation) incorporates the European Convention on Human Rights into UK law.
- **1999** — Immigration and Asylum Act creates the National Asylum Support Service (NASS).

### 21st century

- **2001** — Following the September 11 attacks, the Home Office leads on the Anti-Terrorism, Crime and Security Act.
- **2007** — Ministry of Justice created, taking over responsibility for prisons, probation, and the courts from the Home Office. The Home Office refocuses on immigration, policing, and counter-terrorism.
- **2008** — UK Border Agency (UKBA) formed under the Home Office.
- **2013** — UKBA abolished and functions brought back in-house as UK Visas and Immigration (UKVI) and Immigration Enforcement.
- **2014** — Modern Slavery Act development begins (passed 2015), making the Home Office responsible for tackling human trafficking.
- **2018** — Windrush scandal exposes failings in the Home Office's handling of Commonwealth citizens' documentation. Leads to the Windrush Lessons Learned Review.
- **2020** — EU Settlement Scheme processes over 5 million applications following Brexit.
- **2022** — Home Office leads on the Nationality and Borders Act, restructuring the asylum system.

## Structure today

The Home Office is headquartered at 2 Marsham Street, London. It employs over 30,000 staff and is organised into several directorates:

| Directorate | Responsibility |
|-------------|---------------|
| UK Visas and Immigration (UKVI) | Visa applications, settlement, citizenship |
| Immigration Enforcement | Compliance, removals, detention |
| Border Force | Port and airport controls |
| Office for Security and Counter-Terrorism (OSCT) | National security policy, CONTEST strategy |
| Crime, Policing and Fire Group | Police funding, fire services, serious and organised crime |
| Homeland Security Group | Protective security, CPNI |

## Home Secretaries through the ages

Notable holders of the office include:

- **Robert Peel** (1822–1827, 1828–1830) — founded the Metropolitan Police
- **Sir William Joynson-Hicks** (1924–1929) — oversaw the Equal Franchise Act
- **Herbert Morrison** (1940–1945) — wartime Home Secretary during the Blitz
- **Roy Jenkins** (1965–1967, 1974–1976) — liberalising reforms on race, gender, and sexuality
- **Theresa May** (2010–2016) — longest-serving Home Secretary in over 50 years; created the hostile environment policy
- **Priti Patel** (2019–2022) — oversaw post-Brexit immigration policy
